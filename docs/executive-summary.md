# D5 Executive Summary

This project asks a practical question: when does switching to an electric vehicle make financial sense for a typical driver?

The answer depends on local context. EVs tend to look better when gas prices are high, electricity rates are moderate, the driver has a long commute, and incentives reduce the upfront price difference. EVs look less attractive when electricity is expensive, the EV costs much more than a comparable gas vehicle, or charging access is weak.

The buying assistant estimates annual savings as `<S_annual>` and also checks whether local charging infrastructure looks strong enough for the driver’s commute. That second check matters. A driver can have positive estimated savings but still face a poor ownership experience if home or nearby charging is unreliable.

Recommendation: use the savings estimate as a planning number, then verify the three local facts before buying: home charging, nearby public charging, and the actual electricity rate on the driver’s utility plan.
